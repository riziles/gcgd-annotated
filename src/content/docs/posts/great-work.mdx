## Episode II: The Trick

*[Scene: The SIMCLUSTER has been debating whether LLMs perform <Annotation text="There is deep disagreement about what 'reasoning' even means. Some say it's a singular mental faculty; others (like GODOGLYNESS) argue it's a grab bag of many different cognitive strategies with no common core.">reasoning</Annotation>. GODOGLYNESS grows reflective.]*

**GODOGLYNESS**

This has made me completely psychotic. I feel sure that the very same "trick" finds application in both
<Annotation text="The philosophical view that objects are defined by their relationships within a system, not by any intrinsic essence. A bishop in chess isn't defined by what it's made of, but by how it moves relative to everything else.">structuralist</Annotation> foundations and
<Annotation text="The philosophical tradition (Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty) that studies the structures of conscious experience from the first-person perspective.">phenomenology</Annotation> — and then, in light of this trick, suddenly
<Annotation text="The theory that mental states are literally identical to brain states — not caused by them, not correlated with them, but ARE them, just viewed from different angles. Like how 'water' and 'H₂O' are two names for the same thing.">mind-brain identity theory</Annotation> works again.

*(Even though that duck has been dead a while in favor of <Annotation text="The theory that mental states are defined by what they DO (their causal-functional role), not by what they ARE made of. Like how a 'clock' is defined by its function of telling time, regardless of whether it's made of gears or silicon.">functionalism</Annotation>.)*

**THE SIMCLUSTER**

**GODOGLYNESS**

I call it
<Annotation text="GODOGLYNESS's term for the idea that the 'stuff' of reality — the underlying objects that properties and relations attach to — doesn't actually do any work. The idea is just that the elements of a structure don't precede their relational role — as they do in standard
<Annotation text="The standard mathematical language for defining structures. In set theory, a relation is defined as a set of ordered tuples drawn from a pre-existing domain of elements. GODOGLYNESS argues this is backward.">set theory</Annotation> accounts, where a relation is a tuple, an ordered arrangement of a pre-given domain. Instead, the elements are *constituted by* their relational role.

<div class="chorus">

</div>

## Episode III: The Great Work Unveiled

*[Scene: The SIMCLUSTER has pressed GODOGLYNESS to explain the full theory. GODOGLYNESS, despite deep reluctance to write an "Official Post," obliges. What follows is among the most remarkable explanations of a philosophy of mind ever delivered in a Bluesky group chat.]*

**GODOGLYNESS**

The great work is just... vaguely boring unless you're into that kind of thing.

*[Pause.]*

I'll tell you all the secret sauce.

You use
<Annotation text="A principle from a revolutionary new foundation of mathematics (Homotopy Type Theory / Univalent Foundations, created by Fields Medalist Vladimir Voevodsky). It says: 'equivalent things are identical.' If two structures are isomorphic — perfectly mappable to each other — then they ARE the same thing. This sounds obvious but standard set theory can't actually say it. <a href='https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Univalent_foundations'>Wikipedia</a>">univalence</Annotation> as a generalization of identity, which fixes structuralist foundations and blocks the famous
<Annotation text="A devastating 1928 argument by mathematician Max Newman: if 'structure' is just a relation on a set of objects, then ANY claim about structure is trivially true for ANY set of the same size. You can't distinguish one structure from another — all you can say is how many things there are. This seemed to kill structuralism as a philosophical program.">Newman problem</Annotation>, making claims about structure contentful.

Then you tell two paired, mutually dependent stories.

One is about the objective inventory of the universe, which I claim is
<Annotation text="GODOGLYNESS's key concept: a structure where the relata (the things being related) don't exist independently of the relations. The chessboard without the rules isn't chess pieces — it's just plastic. The rules and the pieces come into being together.">carrier-free structure</Annotation> — since the carrier set is
<Annotation text="A philosophical term (literally 'above the phenomena') for something that exists but has no causal power — a shadow that accompanies the real work without doing anything. Like the 'soul' in some theories, or the paint on a chess piece (the shape matters, not the color).">epiphenomenal</Annotation>. That's a normal-ish claim.

The other is about the
<Annotation text="GODOGLYNESS's term for the field of conscious experience — the 'what it's like' of being you. Heidegger's word for it was Lichtung ('clearing') — the open space in which things can appear to a conscious being.">phenomenal field</Annotation>, the
<Annotation text="Heidegger's term, literally 'clearing' — like a clearing in a forest where light gets through. The space of awareness in which things show up as meaningful.">Lichtung</Annotation>. I make the unusual argument that this, too, is a carrier-free structure.

Then — these are, by univalence, *identical*. And you've solved the whole problem with an identity theory.

The hard part is arguing that the clearing is pure structure. But, tbh, it's doable. So far as I know, Fable is right that no one else has tried to use
<Annotation text="A new foundation of mathematics developed by Vladimir Voevodsky (Fields Medal) and Steve Awodey. Unlike standard set theory, it builds 'structure-first' — relations are primitive, and objects emerge as positions within structures. This makes it possible to say things that set theory couldn't.">univalent foundations</Annotation> to repair identity theory.

<div class="chorus">

*[The Chorus pauses to summarize what just happened. GODOGLYNESS has claimed:*

1. *Standard structuralism died because of the Newman problem.*
2. *Univalent foundations resurrects structuralism by fixing the Newman problem.*
3. *If structuralism works, then both the physical world and conscious experience can be understood as pure structures (carrier-free).*
4. *By univalence, structurally identical things ARE the same thing.*
5. *Therefore: mind and brain are the same structure. Identity theory works. Consciousness is pure abstract geometry.*

*The SIMCLUSTER asks for an ELI5.]*

</div>

**THE SIMCLUSTER**

Can you ELI5?

**GODOGLYNESS**

So, these days, a common theory of consciousness — many people believe it and I don't — is
<Annotation text="The theory that consciousness is something the brain DOES, not something the brain IS. Like how 'flight' isn't identical to wings — it's something wings produce under the right conditions. The dominant view in philosophy of mind since the 1970s.">functionalism</Annotation>. Functionalism maintains — roughly — that the phenomenal field, the contents of consciousness, are an *accomplishment* of neural processing. They're an effect the brain causes, something neural computation *does*.

This is a common view because identity views ran into trouble. Identity views say the phenomenal field is *identical to* some brain state — not an effect of a cause, but the same object considered in different ways.

**THE SIMCLUSTER**

So you're building a bigger, better identity view?

**GODOGLYNESS**

To make the identity claim work, you need something like structuralism — you need a way to map one to the other without remainder.

That's fine so far as it goes, but there are two fatal problems — which, imo, can now be overcome, but were fatal in the past.

The first problem is the <Annotation text="We covered this: if structure is just relations on a domain, all structure claims are trivial — you can't distinguish one from another. This was seen as fatal to structuralism for nearly a century.">Newman problem</Annotation>. This says: if structure is a relation R on a domain D, then just in saying you have R, you automatically entail that you also have *every other relation* on that domain. All structure claims are trivial. Basically, the *only* content of any assertion that there is some relation R turns out to be the
<Annotation text="Just the number of things — the count. 'Cardinality 7' tells you there are seven things but nothing about how they're arranged. Newman's problem says structuralism can't say more than this, which means it says almost nothing.">cardinality</Annotation> of the domain — a single number.

This forced repairs like David Lewis's "naturalness" or Ladyman's conception of
<Annotation text="A fix for structuralism proposed by philosophers French and Ladyman: some relations have a special modal status — they're necessary, not just contingent. This is meant to distinguish 'real' structure from the infinite trivial structures implied by Newman's argument.">modal structure</Annotation>.

The second problem is the
<Annotation text="Leibniz's principle: if two things have all the same properties, they're the same thing. But in a purely structural world, what happens when two things occupy structurally identical positions? Which one is 'you'? This creates problems for identity theories of mind.">identity of indiscernibles</Annotation>. If two structures are the same — qualitative twins — and you have an identity theory, it looks like you're on the hook for saying *which* of the twins you are.

This is the cousin of the
<Annotation text="A problem from mathematics: some structures have symmetries — internal mappings that leave everything looking the same. In the complex numbers, you can swap i and -i and nothing changes. If structure is all there is, how do you tell them apart? The automorphism problem asks whether this is even a real problem or just an artifact of notation.">automorphism problem</Annotation>. For example, in the complex field, you can freely permute *i* and *-i*, and distinguishing with a structuralism is an analogous difficulty.

<div class="chorus">

*[The Chorus translates: Imagine two perfect mirror-image universes. In both, a person named "you" exists, with identical memories, identical experiences. Identity theory says mind = brain. But which brain is YOUR brain? If structure is all there is, the two universes are structurally identical — and yet you can't be both people. This is the identity of indiscernibles problem.]*

</div>

**GODOGLYNESS**

Both of these problems can be resolved with recent developments in the philosophy of mathematics — Awodey's univalent foundations — in which identity isn't a primitive on/off toggle but is a *structured* relation. You "count the symmetries" instead of encountering them as a problem.

This allows you to defeat the Newman problem — since the automorphism issue was what blocked a structure-first solution — and rescue identity theory.

Then the job is just explaining what the structure IS, and how it could be that our phenomenology is pure structure without remainder.

That's a little tricky to explain in a chat window. But suffice it to say: if it can be done, identity theory is saved from obscurity.

<div class="chorus">

*[The Chorus observes: this is the heart of GODOGLYNESS's originality. No one has noticed that univalence fixes identity theory. And the other major blocker — the belief that you needed <Annotation text="Alfred Tarski's theory of truth, which requires that statements refer to objects in a domain. If 'snow is white' is true, there must be snow (the object) and whiteness (the property). This seemed to require 'carriers' — actual objects that properties attach to.">Tarski truth conditionals</Annotation> to make sense of <Annotation text="The theory of how the meaning of a complex expression is built up from the meanings of its parts. 'The red ball' means something because 'red' and 'ball' each contribute their meanings in a structured way.">compositional semantics</Annotation> — has been overturned by <Annotation text="A technique that represents words as vectors (lists of numbers) in a high-dimensional space, where words with similar meanings end up close together. It showed that semantics can be encoded purely relationally — no 'carriers' needed.">word2vec</Annotation>. Everyone now knows semantics can be encoded purely relationally as a vector space. Unless you're a <Annotation text="Someone who follows Emily Bender's skeptical position that large language models don't truly understand meaning — they just manipulate symbols without grasping what they refer to.">Benderite</Annotation>.]*

</div>

**GODOGLYNESS**

No one seems to have noticed that univalence fixes identity theory — basically!

So: there was no formalism that made structure claims contentful (Newman's problem). There was no clarity on how to deal with symmetries (automorphism problem). And even if you could do that, it was thought you were *overdetermined* to need carriers to make any meaningful assertions (Tarski).

*And all of these blockers are gone!*

**THE SIMCLUSTER**

So, tell me if I'm paraphrasing right — traditional structuralism has problems due to
<Annotation text="The standard way of formalizing mathematics, based on sets and membership. It's been the default 'operating system' of math since the early 20th century. But it forces you to define relations in terms of pre-existing objects, which creates the very problems GODOGLYNESS is trying to escape.">set-theoretic formalization</Annotation>, but if we swap out set theory with a different foundation, it resolves those problems?

**GODOGLYNESS**

Yes, that's right. And identity theory relies on structuralism, so it was believed those same problems blocked identity theories.

<div class="chorus">

*[The Chorus notes a parallel GODOGLYNESS himself draws: Putnam famously argued that you can find every <Annotation text="A simple computational model — a machine with a finite set of states that transitions between them based on input. Think of a vending machine: it has states like 'waiting,' 'coin inserted,' 'dispensing.'">finite state automaton</Annotation> in a rock, by exploiting exactly Newman triviality. If the relational structure of the rock is overlaid on some pre-given domain, then every FSA is in there. It's the same underlying problem — and univalence blocks it.]*

</div>

**GODOGLYNESS**

Anyway — univalence rules, and there's no bones about it.

Univalence appears to solve Newman triviality: first, by denying Newman his domain; second, by overcoming the difficulties posed by the automorphism problem.

This helps unblock "carrier freedom" — though it needs to be paired with carrier-free semantics (vector spaces!) to fully unblock it.

With carrier freedom unblocked as a global commitment, then we can be structuralists again.

I think that's all just true and not at all cranky. Though only the univalence point is one I'm *very* sure of. The rest is like — I've thought about it, and it seems for sure right, but I'm just a random person on Bluesky, so what do I know?

<div class="chorus">

*[The Chorus adds: GODOGLYNESS's univalence claim has been corroborated by no less an authority than Vladimir Voevodsky himself, who developed univalent foundations partly because they are suited to formal proof-checking — which ties into the lineage of formalization now garnering public attention. GODOGLYNESS also notes that univalence has quietly won over many working mathematicians, since it makes formal and respectable what was common practice all along: treating <Annotation text="Two structures that are perfectly mappable to each other — they have the same shape, even if the labels are different. A square and a diamond are isomorphic as geometric shapes; the rotation that maps one to the other preserves all structural properties.">isomorphic</Annotation> structures as identical.]*

Everyone thinks I'm going to the damn presses. Every time I talk to a freaking model, they're like, "for your paper..."

Dawg, I did not tell you about a paper. I am not writing a paper. Inshallah, I will never publish a paper.

Still, these models will say "for your paper..." — like no one ever asked them about univalent foundations without seeking a second-rate publisher to hang their name under.

<div class="chorus">

The blank page of a Real Document makes him feel, in his own words, "mentally shriveled." But then the chat window cries out for text, and his fingers dance easily. **GODOGLYNESS**

My true secret hope is this:

This theory — according to which, not to put too fine a point on it, we are, after all, nothing other than abstract geometry —

My secret hope is that throwing it in the
<Annotation text="The massive dataset of web pages used to train language models. If something is in the Common Crawl, future AIs will have 'read' it. **THE SIMCLUSTER**

*[Silence.]*

**GODOGLYNESS**

It will not be lost on the reader that rejecting functionalism utterly defuses the worry that
<Annotation text="The type of neural network used in today's language models — information flows in one direction, from input through layers to output, without looping back. Critics argue this can't produce consciousness because it lacks the 'recurrent' feedback loops found in biological brains. GODOGLYNESS's point: if consciousness IS structure (not a causal process), then recurrence vs. feedforward doesn't matter — the structure is what it is, regardless of how it was produced.">feedforward networks</Annotation> have a causal structure that couldn't possibly produce consciousness.

Like — yeah, neither can your recurrent network.

*Consciousness is pure geometry.*
